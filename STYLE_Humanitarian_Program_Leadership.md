**Target Audience**: Country Directors, Program Managers, Field Coordinators, M&E Leads, Operations Managers
**Context**: Program implementation guidance, field operations, M&E frameworks, coordination mechanisms, operational learning
**Analysis Date**: November 24, 2025

## Executive Summary: Style Adaptation for Program Leadership

This style guide adapts Tom Jordan's core voice for humanitarian program leadership audiences. The adaptation emphasizes **operational effectiveness over strategic vision**, **field team enablement over organizational policy**, and **practical implementation in complex, resource-constrained environments**. Writing for program leaders requires understanding the daily realities of delivering humanitarian assistance—managing teams, coordinating partners, navigating access constraints, reporting to donors, and adapting rapidly to changing contexts.

**Key shifts from base style**:
- **Operational focus**: Field implementation, team management, logistics coordination
- **Practical problem-solving**: Addressing concrete challenges field teams face daily
- **M&E and reporting**: Donor frameworks, indicators, evidence collection
- **Partnership coordination**: Working with local organizations, UN agencies, clusters
- **Staff wellbeing**: Team safety, stress management, capacity development
- **Context adaptation**: Specific guidance for varied operating environments

### Paragraph Construction

**Target length**: 3-5 sentences (with operational specificity)
**Pattern**: Field Challenge-Impact-Practical Solution-Implementation Guidance

**Example**:
> "Distribution teams in camp settings report that beneficiary verification using paper lists takes 4-6 hours per distribution, creating long wait times that expose women and children to sun exposure and security risks. The verification process is also vulnerable to errors and fraud, with duplicate registrations difficult to identify. Basic mobile verification tools using beneficiary photos and unique IDs can reduce verification time to 30-45 minutes per distribution while improving accuracy. Implementation requires 2-3 smartphones per distribution team ($300), three days of staff training, and integration with your existing beneficiary registration database—with technical support available from HQ if needed."

**What's different**:
- Specific operational detail (4-6 hours, long wait times)
- Names actual field consequences (sun exposure, security risks, fraud)
- Quantified improvement (30-45 minutes)
- Practical resource requirements ($300, 3 days training)
- Implementation specifics (integration with existing systems, HQ support)

## IV. PRACTICAL IMPLEMENTATION FOCUS

### Step-by-Step Operational Guidance

**Detailed implementation steps**:

```markdown
## Implementation Timeline: 8-Week Rollout

### Weeks 1-2: Preparation and Training

**Week 1: Setup and Configuration**
- Day 1-2: HQ technical team configures system for your context
  - Upload beneficiary database (coordinate with M&E officer)
  - Set up user accounts for distribution team
  - Configure for offline functionality
- Day 3-5: Devices and equipment procurement
  - Purchase 3-4 smartphones (see equipment specs in Annex A)
  - Obtain protective cases and portable chargers
  - Arrange solar charging if grid power unreliable
  - Test connectivity in distribution sites

**Week 2: Staff Training and Pilot**
- Day 1-2: Training of trainers for team leaders (2-day workshop)
- Day 3-4: Field team training (hands-on with devices)
- Day 5: Dry run with 50 beneficiaries, troubleshoot issues

### Weeks 3-4: Pilot Implementation
[Continue with specific week-by-week guidance]

### Common Issues and Solutions
- **Issue**: Devices run out of battery during distributions
  **Solution**: Bring 2 portable chargers per device; rotate usage
- **Issue**: Photos fail to capture clearly in bright sunlight
  **Solution**: Have beneficiaries stand in shaded area; train staff on proper lighting
```

**What makes this effective for program leaders**:
- Specific day-by-day timeline
- Named responsible roles (M&E officer, team leader)
- Practical procurement details
- Context adaptations (solar charging, unreliable power)
- Troubleshooting from actual field experience

### Resource and Logistics Details

**Practical resource specifications**:

```markdown
## Resource Requirements

**Equipment** (per distribution team of 5 people):
- 3× smartphones: Samsung Galaxy A series or equivalent
  - Minimum specs: Android 10+, 64GB storage, 4GB RAM
  - Cost: $250-300 per device
  - Protective case required (rugged, waterproof)
- 3× portable battery packs (20,000 mAh minimum)
- 1× portable solar charger (optional, for areas with limited electricity)
- Total equipment cost: ~$1,200 per team

**Training**:
- 2-day TOT for team leaders: $500 (venue, materials, refreshments)
- 2-day field team training: $300
- Ongoing coaching: 4 hours/week for first month (existing staff time)

**Operational costs** (monthly):
- Mobile data: $20/device/month = $60/month
- Device maintenance/replacement fund: $100/month
- Troubleshooting support: HQ staff time (no additional cost)

**Total first-year cost**: ~$2,400 (equipment) + $800 (training) + $1,920 (12 months data) = **$5,120 per distribution team**

**Cost per beneficiary**: Assuming 5,000 beneficiaries served = **$1.02 per beneficiary**
```

## VI. PARTNER COORDINATION AND COMMUNITY ENGAGEMENT

### Partnership Implementation

**Local partner collaboration**:

```markdown
## Implementing with Local Partners

### Partnership Approach

**Context**: You're implementing this with three local partner organizations who conduct distributions in hard-to-reach areas.

**Coordination mechanism**:
1. **Monthly partner meeting**: Review implementation progress, troubleshoot challenges
2. **Joint training**: Train your staff and partner staff together (builds relationships)
3. **Shared resources**: Partners can use your devices if theirs fail; you share lessons learned
4. **Unified reporting**: Use same M&E framework so data is comparable

**Technical support model**:
- You provide: Initial training, troubleshooting support, device repair/replacement
- Partners provide: Community mobilization, cultural adaptation, local knowledge
- Joint: Problem-solving for implementation challenges, sharing best practices

**Common partnership challenges**:
| Challenge | Solution |
|-----------|----------|
| Partner staff turnover disrupts training continuity | Record training videos in local language; designate "super users" who can train new staff |
| Different tech capacity across partners | Tier support: more intensive support for partners with less tech experience |
| Data sharing concerns | Clear data-sharing MOU; partner controls their beneficiary data; you only aggregate for reporting |
| Unequal resource access | Rotate device distribution to ensure equity; budget for partner equipment needs |
```

### Community Engagement

**Beneficiary communication and participation**:
> "Before introducing the digital verification system, conduct community consultations in each distribution site. Explain what will change and why (faster, more accurate, more dignified). Address concerns, especially about data privacy. Involve community leaders and beneficiary committees in designing the rollout. After initial distributions, gather beneficiary feedback through focus groups (disaggregate by gender, age, disability) and adapt based on what you hear. This builds trust and ensures the system serves community needs, not just operational efficiency."

## VII. STAFF CAPACITY AND WELLBEING

### Team Training and Development

**Capacity building approach**:
> "Plan for 2-3 weeks of reduced distribution activity during initial training and pilot phases—your team is learning a new system while maintaining regular program delivery, which creates stress. Use a TOT model: train 2-3 team leaders intensively, then they train their teams with your support. Build in regular coaching sessions during the first month; most learning happens through problem-solving real issues, not classroom training. Celebrate small wins with your team as they master the system."

### Staff Wellbeing Considerations

**Workload and stress management**:
> "Introducing new systems during active program implementation adds to already-heavy workloads. Be realistic about what your team can absorb. If you're in a rapid-onset emergency, delay implementation until operations stabilize. If staff are managing trauma from recent security incidents, add more training time and support. Build in peer learning sessions where staff can share frustrations and solutions—this reduces stress and improves implementation. Monitor for burnout and adjust timeline if needed."

## VIII. CONTEXT-SPECIFIC ADAPTATIONS

### Operating Environment Variations

**Adaptation by context**:

```markdown
## Context-Specific Guidance

### Urban Refugee Settings
**Challenges**: High density, diverse nationalities/languages, fluid populations
**Adaptations**:
- Ensure system supports multiple languages for UI and photo captions
- Update beneficiary lists more frequently (weekly vs. monthly)
- Higher device needs due to volume (5-6 smartphones vs. 3)

### Rural/Remote Locations
**Challenges**: Limited connectivity, electricity access, device security risks
**Adaptations**:
- Prioritize offline functionality; sync when return to office
- Solar chargers essential
- Rugged device cases and secure storage
- Longer distribution cycles due to travel time

### Conflict-Affected Areas
**Challenges**: Security risks, checkpoint confiscation, data sensitivity
**Adaptations**:
- No beneficiary photos stored on devices; use unique IDs only
- Remote wipe capability if device confiscated
- Encryption mandatory
- Physical security protocols (disguised transport, etc.)

### Camp Settings
**Challenges**: Large volumes, controlled environment, registration coordination
**Adaptations**:
- Integrate with UNHCR registration (ProGres) if possible
- Set up dedicated verification stations (parallel lanes)
- Coordinate with camp management on schedules
```

### Seasonal and Cultural Factors

**Calendar and cultural adaptation**:
> "In Muslim-majority contexts, distributions during Ramadan require schedule adjustments to avoid midday heat when beneficiaries are fasting. In agricultural regions, planting and harvest seasons affect when beneficiaries are available for registration or distributions—coordinate with community leaders on timing. In areas with rainy seasons, waterproof device cases become essential and access roads may be impassable, requiring pre-positioning of supplies."

## IX. TROUBLESHOOTING AND ESCALATION

### Common Implementation Challenges

**Field problem-solving**:

```markdown
## Troubleshooting Guide

### Technical Issues

**Problem**: Device won't sync data back to central database
**Quick fixes**:
- Check if mobile data is active and has credit
- Try connecting to WiFi if available
- Restart device and retry
**Escalate to HQ if**: Problem persists after 24 hours
**Workaround**: Continue collecting data offline; sync later

**Problem**: Battery dies mid-distribution
**Quick fixes**:
- Use portable charger
- Rotate to backup device
**Prevention**: Start day with full charges; bring 2 backup batteries per device
**Escalate to HQ if**: Batteries not holding charge (replacement needed)

### Operational Issues

**Problem**: Beneficiaries concerned about photos being taken
**Response approach**:
- Explain photos are only for verification, not shared
- Show them their photo is only visible to distribution team
- Offer alternative: fingerprint or signature if culturally more acceptable
- Document concerns and escalate to Protection team if pattern emerges

**Problem**: Partner organizations hesitant to adopt system
**Response approach**:
- Understand specific concerns (cost, complexity, data control?)
- Offer additional training and hands-on support
- Start with most tech-confident partner; use them as example
- Don't mandate—build buy-in through demonstration
**Escalate to HQ if**: Partner relationship strain; may need leadership discussion
```

### When to Escalate

**Escalation guidance**:
- **Technical issues**: If unresolved after 24-48 hours, contact HQ IT support
- **Security concerns**: Immediate escalation if device confiscated or data breach suspected
- **Protection issues**: If beneficiaries report harm or discrimination, escalate to Protection Coordinator
- **Partnership conflicts**: If threatens program delivery, involve Country Director
- **Donor compliance**: If system creates compliance risks, escalate to M&E and Compliance teams

## XI. EXAMPLE TRANSFORMATIONS

### Base Style (IT Professional Audience):
> "Zero-trust architecture requires authenticating and authorizing every access request, regardless of network location. Implementation involves multiple technical components working together: identity verification, device assessment, and policy enforcement."

### Humanitarian Program Leadership Adaptation:
> "In field operations spanning multiple locations with various staff and partner organizations accessing beneficiary data, ensuring the right people have the right access without exposing sensitive information is a persistent challenge. During SIMS deployment for Hurricane Beryl in Grenada, coordinating information management across damaged island infrastructure while maintaining data security required careful access controls. Traditional password-based systems are vulnerable when staff use shared devices, work from internet cafés in disaster zones, or access data over unsecured networks—as we saw coordinating across five states during Hurricanes Helene/Milton with 1,900+ responders. An access control system that verifies both user identity (through two-factor authentication via SMS) and device authorization (only approved devices can access) protects beneficiary data while allowing field teams the access they need. Your M&E team can implement this in 2-3 weeks with minimal cost ($0—using free authentication services), and it integrates with your existing systems. Experience from IFRC surge deployments including Ebola response in DRC shows no data breaches while maintaining field team productivity even in challenging environments."

**What changed**:
- Technical term ("zero-trust") removed; concept explained in field operations terms
- Opens with specific operational context (multiple locations, staff, partners)
- Field challenges named (shared devices, internet cafés, unsecured networks)
- Practical implementation timeline (2-3 weeks)
- Cost specified ($0—removes budget barrier)
- Integration with existing systems emphasized
- Cites humanitarian country programs (not tech companies)
- M&E team named as implementation lead (operational ownership)
- Success metric is field-relevant (no breaches, maintained productivity)

## Conclusion: Writing for Program Implementation

Writing for humanitarian program leadership requires **operational specificity**, **practical implementation guidance**, and **deep respect for the realities field teams navigate daily**. Program leaders need concrete, actionable guidance they can adapt to their specific contexts, not high-level strategy or technical abstractions.

**Core principles for program leadership writing**:
1. **Operationally specific**: Actual steps, timelines, resources, roles
2. **Field team enabling**: Remove obstacles, provide support, build capacity
3. **M&E integrated**: Indicators, data collection, reporting from the start
4. **Partnership oriented**: Coordinate with local organizations, cluster mechanisms
5. **Context adaptable**: Guidance flexible for different operating environments
6. **Staff-centered**: Capacity, wellbeing, workload considered
7. **Problem-solving**: Troubleshoot challenges, escalate appropriately

Tom Jordan's practitioner-educator voice translates powerfully to program leadership audiences because it already emphasizes **frameworks as practical guides** (not abstract theory), **step-by-step approaches**, **acknowledgment of complexity**, and **respect for implementers' expertise**. The adaptation intensifies operational detail, M&E integration, and field team perspective.

**The program leadership version of your voice is**: The experienced field colleague who has implemented in challenging contexts, understands the daily pressures program teams face, provides practical operational guidance, supports staff capacity and wellbeing, and helps navigate the complexity of delivering quality humanitarian assistance under resource constraints and access challenges.

## XIII. HISTORICAL CONTEXT: LESSONS FROM THE FIELD

### Learning from Past Responses

*This section is optional enrichment. You don't need to know this history to manage programs well. But understanding where our standards and principles came from can help when you're making tough decisions in the field.*

The standards we work with—Sphere, CHS, cluster system, localization commitments—weren't created in headquarters. They emerged from field responses where things went wrong, where we learned hard lessons, where program teams said "there has to be a better way."

**Consider these connections** (when facing similar challenges):

- **Rwanda genocide (1994)**: In the aftermath, agencies flooded in with assistance but no coordination, no quality standards, unequal distribution. Some camps had 10 WASH programs while others had none. From this chaos came **Sphere Standards** (1997)—minimum standards ensuring everyone receives quality assistance regardless of which agency responds. When you're coordinating distributions or WASH services, Sphere isn't bureaucracy—it's remembering Rwanda and ensuring quality.

- **Tsunami response (2004)** and **Haiti earthquake (2010)**: Massive international response but poor coordination with local organizations, inadequate community consultation, some agencies doing harm through poor practice. Local organizations were sidelined even though they knew context better. From these failures came the **Core Humanitarian Standard** (2014) and the **Grand Bargain commitment to localization** (2016). When you're working with local partners, these frameworks remind us: local leadership isn't optional, it's essential.

- **Syria response** (2011-present): International agencies couldn't access many areas, but local organizations operated throughout. They proved that local and national organizations can lead complex responses—if international actors support rather than control. This strengthened the **localization agenda**: funding local organizations directly, national staff in leadership, community-led assessment and design. When you're program manager, putting national staff in lead roles isn't just good practice—it's applying lessons from Syria.

- **Ebola West Africa** (2014-2015): Technical experts arrived with protocols that didn't account for burial practices, family care customs, or community decision-making. Communities initially resisted because programs violated their values and weren't designed with their input. Only when response shifted to community-led approaches with technical support did it become effective. When you're implementing health or protection programs, community consultation isn't a checkbox—it's operational necessity learned from Ebola.

**Practical application**: When you're facing challenges in your program—coordination failures, quality concerns, community resistance, partner tensions—these historical lessons offer guidance. The standards exist because program teams before you faced the same challenges and learned what works.

**For the Stoically inclined** (completely optional):

Marcus Aurelius wrote his *Meditations* while leading military campaigns—notes to himself on staying grounded while managing impossible circumstances. Every program manager knows this: maintaining integrity under pressure, making decisions with incomplete information, supporting your team when everything is hard.

- **Dichotomy of control**: You control your effort, your principles, how you treat your team. You don't control donor funding, security situations, government access permissions, or weather. Focus on what's in your hands—program quality, staff support, partner relationships, beneficiary dignity. Release what you can't control.

- **Sympatheia** (we're all connected): Your program affects beneficiaries, who affect communities, which affects regional stability, which affects global systems. Small acts matter. How you treat the most junior national staff member affects team culture, which affects program quality, which affects beneficiary outcomes. Everything connects.

- **Justice**: Treating all people fairly—beneficiaries, staff, partners. When exhausted and under pressure, this is hard. The Stoics taught that justice is the highest virtue, enacted through small daily choices. Equal treatment in distributions, fair compensation for national staff, respectful partnership with local organizations—this is Stoic justice in action.

You don't need philosophy to do this work well. But sometimes when you're exhausted and facing impossible choices, knowing that people have navigated this before can help. Focus on what you control, treat everyone with justice, accept what you cannot change, keep going. That's both Stoicism and good program management.

## XIV. ANTI-OPPRESSIVE PRACTICE IN FIELD OPERATIONS

### National Staff Leadership and Expatriate Role

**Be honest about power dynamics**: As program manager, you need to actively work against colonial patterns that persist in humanitarian work. This isn't about feeling guilty—it's about building effective programs that strengthen local capacity rather than creating dependency.

**Questions for your program operations:**

**Who leads your program?**
- Is your senior team mostly expatriates or mostly national staff?
- Are national staff in technical/coordination roles or only "implementing"?
- Who makes strategic decisions—expats in office or national staff who know context?
- When you leave (eventually), will the program collapse or continue under national leadership?

**How do you work with local partners?**
- Do you call them "implementing partners" (subcontractors) or "partners" (equals)?
- Do they get real input into program design or just implement your plan?
- Does funding go directly to them or only through you with heavy reporting requirements?
- Do you build their capacity or extract their capacity for your programs?

**How do you interact with communities?**
- Do beneficiary committees actually influence programming or just rubber-stamp your decisions?
- Do you ask what communities want or tell them what you'll provide?
- Are beneficiaries "recipients" of aid or "participants" in recovery?
- Do you collect stories with consent and compensation, or extract them for fundraising?

**How does your team operate?**
- Are expatriate salaries 5-10x national staff for same work? (Common, but unjust)
- Do expats stay in guesthouseswhile national staff commute from difficult housing?
- Do expats get R&R and danger pay while national staff face same risks without benefits?
- Are international consultants paid more than national experts with deeper context knowledge?

### Practical Anti-Oppressive Approaches

**Shift expatriate role from leader to supporter:**

❌ **Expatriate as Country Director**: External person leads, national staff implement
✅ **National staff as Country Director**: Local leadership with expat technical support

❌ **Expat decides program priorities**: "We know what this community needs"
✅ **Community and local staff decide**: "We support priorities they've identified"

❌ **Expatriate-designed programs**: Created at HQ, rolled out to field
✅ **Field-designed programs**: Created by national staff and communities, HQ provides technical support

**Partnership as genuine collaboration:**

❌ **Local partner as subcontractor**: "Implement our program; report to us"
✅ **Local partner as co-designer**: "What should we do together? How can we support your priorities?"

❌ **Heavy reporting burden**: Monthly narrative reports, photos for donor reports, endless Excel sheets
✅ **Reasonable accountability**: Quarterly reports, shared learning, mutual feedback

❌ **Funding only for your activities**: Partner does what serves your program
✅ **Core funding included**: Partner institutional capacity strengthened, not just your project

**Beneficiary dignity in daily operations:**

❌ **Distribution line photos for fundraising**: Long lines of people waiting, reinforces helplessness
✅ **Community agency photos**: People organizing, rebuilding, leading—with consent

❌ **"Vulnerable beneficiaries need our help"**: Deficit framing, savior narrative
✅ **"Communities surviving extraordinary circumstances; we provide support they requested"**: Asset-based, dignity-preserving

❌ **Take beneficiary stories for reports**: Extract stories without consent or compensation
✅ **Co-create stories with consent**: Compensation, review, approval before publication

### How This Shows Up in Your Daily Work

**In distributions:**
- Beneficiaries waiting for hours in sun → Dignity violation
- Solution: Appointment times, shaded waiting areas, water provided
- Treat people as you'd want your family treated

**In assessments:**
- External staff asking questions, community answering → Extractive
- Better: Community-led assessment with your technical support
- Community defines needs, you provide methodology and resources

**In team meetings:**
- Expats speaking, national staff taking notes → Colonial hierarchy
- Better: National staff leading meetings, expats contributing expertise
- Whose context knowledge matters more?

**In reporting:**
- "We provided X to beneficiaries" → Centers the agency
- Better: "Communities accessed X through locally-led distribution" → Centers community agency

This isn't about political correctness—it's about effectiveness. Programs led by people who understand context, speak local languages, and will remain after you leave are more effective, more sustainable, and more just.

## XV. CROSS-LINGUISTIC OPERATIONS: FIELD NECESSITY

### Language Accessibility in Field Operations

**This is operational necessity, not preference.** Your field teams work in multiple languages simultaneously. Your local partners operate in local languages. Beneficiaries speak languages you may not. Community health workers translate technical guidance in real-time. When SOPs and guidelines are full of idioms and unclear language, field staff misunderstand—and that affects beneficiary safety.

**Your operational context probably includes:**
- National staff working in 2-4 languages daily
- Local partners whose first language isn't English
- Beneficiaries speaking local languages, dialects, or minority languages
- Technical guidance written in English at HQ needing field translation
- Coordination meetings where not everyone shares a fluent language
- Distribution scripts that must be translated for community mobilizers

**When your SOP says "flag issues to your supervisor"**, does that translate clearly? Or does it become confusing?

**When beneficiary messaging says "keep your eyes peeled for Ebola symptoms"**, what happens when community health workers translate that literally?

### Writing SOPs and Guidance for Translation

**Field teams need crystal-clear operational guidance:**

**Standard Operating Procedure language:**
- One instruction per sentence
- Active voice: "Report to your supervisor immediately" not "Issues should be reported"
- Specific verbs: "Stop distribution" not "hold off on distribution"
- No idioms, no metaphors, no cultural references

**Bad SOP (idiom-heavy, unclear when translated):**
> "If things go south during the distribution, don't try to tough it out. Flag your team leader ASAP and they'll reach out to the program manager to get things back on track."

**When translated to French → Swahili → back to English:**
> "If things go to the south during distribution, don't try to be tough. Put up a flag for your team leader quickly and they will extend their hand to program manager to return things to the path."

*Unclear and potentially dangerous in an emergency distribution.*

**Good SOP (clear, translates accurately):**
> "If problems occur during distribution, stop immediately. Report the problem to your team leader right away. The team leader will contact the program manager for guidance."

**When translated through multiple languages, meaning preserves.**

### Beneficiary Communication That Translates

**Community mobilization scripts must work in local languages:**

**Bad (idioms and unclear language):**
> "Registration is right around the corner. Don't miss the boat—get your family signed up or you'll be left high and dry when distributions roll out."

**Good (clear, direct, translates well):**
> "Registration begins on Monday, November 27 at the community center. Register your family between November 27-30. Only registered families will receive assistance. Come early—registration closes Friday at 5:00 PM."

### Operational Idioms to Avoid in Field Guidance

| Avoid | Use Instead | Why |
|-------|-------------|-----|
| "Roll out the program" | "Begin the program" or "start implementation" | "Roll out" unclear in translation |
| "Kick off distributions" | "Start distributions" or "begin distributions" | Sports metaphor |
| "Touch base with partners" | "Meet with partners" or "contact partners" | Vague idiom |
| "Red flag this issue" | "Report this issue immediately" or "urgent: report this" | Multiple meanings |
| "Ballpark number" | "Approximate number" | Baseball reference |
| "Get the ball rolling" | "Begin" or "start the process" | Sports metaphor |
| "Circle back" | "Return to this topic" or "discuss again" | Unclear action |
| "Deep dive" | "Detailed analysis" or "thorough examination" | Metaphorical |
| "Low-hanging fruit" | "Easily achievable priorities" | Agricultural metaphor |
| "Move the needle" | "Create improvement" or "make progress" | Unclear idiom |

### Partner Coordination Across Languages

**Working with local partners who operate in local languages:**

**Include translation costs in partner budgets**: Don't require partners to translate everything to English at their expense. That's extractive.

**Provide key documents in local languages**: SOPs, M&E tools, donor requirements—translate them. Don't burden partners.

**Use interpreters in coordination meetings**: Not everyone is fluent in English. Interpretation ensures full participation.

**Value multilingual staff**: National staff working in 3+ languages are providing enormous cognitive labor. Recognize this.

### Clear Writing Saves Lives

**Example: Public health messaging in Ebola response**

**Bad (unclear, dangerous when translated):**
> "Keep your eyes peeled for symptoms. If anyone's running a fever or looks under the weather, flag them right away before this thing snowballs."

**Good (clear, life-saving when translated):**
> "Watch for these symptoms: fever, vomiting, diarrhea, unexplained bleeding. Report anyone with these symptoms immediately to the health supervisor. Early reporting prevents disease spread."

The second version translates accurately across languages. In a disease outbreak, this clarity saves lives.

### Practical Steps for Your Program

**SOPs and guidance documents:**
1. Write in clear, simple English (one idea per sentence, active voice, no idioms)
2. Have national staff review: "Is this clear? Does it translate well?"
3. Translate key SOPs to working languages of your field teams
4. Test: Have bilingual staff translate and back-translate—does meaning preserve?

**Beneficiary messaging:**
1. Draft in simple, literal language
2. Work with community mobilizers to adapt for cultural context
3. Test with small group before wide distribution
4. Provide messaging in all languages spoken in target communities

**Partnership documents:**
1. Translate MOU, reporting templates, M&E tools to partner languages
2. Budget for translation costs
3. Allow partners to report in their language (you pay for translation)
4. Coordination meetings in local language with interpretation if needed
